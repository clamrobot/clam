Continuous Latent Action Models: Towards Capable Robot Policies From Videos

Paper Code

We introduce continuous latent action models (CLAM), a scalable method for learning capable robot policies from unlabeled video data.

Abstract

The standard approach to training robot policies is using imitation learning on large amounts of action-labeled expert demonstrations, which are expensive to collect and thus not a scalable solution. One promising idea is to leverage Internet-scale videos and learn latent action labels in an unsupervised way. However, as we will show, the existing methods for this idea struggle with complex robotic tasks which require fine-grained motions. Motivated by our findings, we introduce \emph{continuous latent action models} (CLAM), a scalable method for learning capable robot policies from unlabeled video data. CLAM learns continuous latent action labels and improves grounding to true environment actions by jointly training an action decoder on only a small amount of action-labeled data that is not necessarily optimal. We demonstrate on various continuous control benchmarks in DMControl (locomotion), MetaWorld and CALVIN (manipulation) that CLAM significantly outperforms prior state-of-the-art methods, remarkably with a $3 \times$ improvement in task success rate compared to the best baseline. Furthermore, we see that in many scenarios, CLAM nearly matches the performance of a BC method that has access to the true action labels. This work demonstrates the potential of leveraging abundant unlabeled video data to train scalable robot policies, reducing the reliance on costly expert-labeled demonstrations.
